The health benefits of Yoga

Yoga is a safe and effective way to increase your physical activity.
Yoga helps with increasing strength, balance and flexibility.
Yoga is known to be beneficial to people with heart disease, high blood pressure, people suffering from stress and depression.
As well as people with lower back pain or aches and pains.

A Brief History of Yoga

Yoga has a vast and rich, 5,000-year history. This is because it is such a multifaceted practice, Yoga has in the past intertwined with philosophy, religion and exercise. There are suggestions that the principles of yoga stem from a Bronze age civilization in the northwestern regions of South Asia, the Indus Valley civilization. Whilst others have noted references to Yoga between 500 BCE and 200 BCE. This time scale is when the philological ideas of Hinduism and Buddhism were taking shape.
During the Vedic Period era the four ancient scriptures (Rigveda, Yajurveda, Samaveda and Atharvaveda) were created, these are the holy writings of Brahmanism. It is in these scriptures that the Sanskrit word "Yuj" (the root of the word Yoga) is first mentioned.
During the pre-classical period era the 700-verse Bhagavad Gita was created, this is the most famous of Hindu texts and is credited as being the oldest known yoga scripture. Although these teachings are dedicated entirely to Yoga as it existed at the time, very little is devoted to the practical elements of the practice.

Classical

2,000 years ago an Indian sage and author of several Sanskrit texts, Patanjali structured the practice of yoga and through documenting his work, the Yoga Sutras, were able to follow his work. Patanjali’s Yoga Sutras are credited with being the foundation of classic yoga as we know it. Pantanjali set out as a means for living a life of purpose and meaning the following:

  • Asana – relates to the practice of physical postures, these are the most well known of the eight limbs
  • Dharana – an aid to concentration, a way of drawing the senses inward
  • Dhyana – the practice of meditation
  • Niyama – is the correct conduct towards oneself, and a means of aiding personal growth
  • Pranayama – a method of controlling the breath
  • Pratyahara – a way of controlling the senses
  • Samadhi – the combination of mediation and the subject of meditation, this is often related to obtaining enlightenment
  • Yama – these are the ethical rules relating to the conduct towards others or social discipline

Post-Classical

The West was introduced to Yoga in the early 19th century and its Eastern teachings and philosophies. The early Yoga philosophies and teachings have become westernised over time. Through this period many gurus and teachers of Yoga travelled West to promote and teach the ways of Yoga. Swami Sivananda was one such guru, he was responsible for writing over 200 books on yoga and its philosophies.
Two other well-known names from this period include: Tirumalai Krishnamacharya, who is referred to as the father of modern Yoya, was one of the most influential yoga teachers of the 20th century, and the 'giggling guru' 'known for regularly laughing during TV interviews' Maharishi Mahesh Yogi, who developed Transcendental Meditation. A technique exercised by over 5 million people worldwide.

Modern Yoga

This is the form of Yoga practised in studios, gyms and homes worldwide today. This form of Yoga has just as complex lineage as the forms preceding it. Multiple current styles of modern Yoga are based on Hatha Yoga. There are several prominent styles of Yoga you may have heard of or be familiar with, they include:

  • Ashtanga
  • Bikram - more commonly known as hot yoga
  • Hatha
  • Integral
  • Iyengar
  • Kundalini
  • Sivananda
  • Viniyoga
  • Vinyasa

Yoga has developed and evolved as a practice and form of exercise over the last 5000 years and maybe considered an organic form. It is likely to continue to develop, grow and evolve for thousands of years to come.

A Guide to Foundational Poses

It can be helpful to familiarize yourself with some of the main foundational poses that most physical practices use.
Check out this list of poses with alignment cues that you can practice in the comfort of your own home.
Here are 9 foundational yoga poses for you to practice and familiarize yourself with.

Warrior-I Pose

  1. Stand up straight and step your right foot back.
  2. Keep your front foot pointing straight forward and position your back foot at approximately a 45-degree angle.
  3. Position your feet hip-width apart so you’re able to square your hips to the front of the mat.
  4. Bend into your front knee. Make sure your knee is directly above your ankle, or behind it.
  5. Keep your back leg strong.
  6. Raise your arms up straight above your head and relax your shoulders.
  7. Hold for 5 breaths before switching to the other side.

Warrior-II Pose

  1. Stand up straight and step your right foot back.
  2. Keep your front foot pointing straight forward. Position your back foot at a little less than a 90-degree angle.
  3. Align your front heel with the arch of your back foot
  4. Have your hips turned toward the side of the mat.
  5. Bend into your front knee so your knee is directly above your ankle, or behind it, ensuring the kneecap is tracking over the middle toe
  6. Keep your back leg strong
  7. Raise your arms up parallel with the ground
  8. Relax your shoulders.
  9. Hold for 5 breaths before coming to the other side.

Cobra

  1. Lie on your stomach with your legs straight.
  2. Firm up the muscles in your legs and have your feet hip-width apart and your toes pointing behind you.
  3. Push down through your pubic bone to avoid collapsing into the lower portion of the spine.
  4. Place your weight onto your forearms as you lift your chest away from the ground.
  5. Make sure that your neck is long as you look straight ahead.
  6. Hold for 5 breaths.

Downward Dog

  1. Come onto your hands and knees.
  2. Straighten your arms and relax your upper back between the shoulder blades.
  3. Keeping your knees bent, lengthen your knees and lift your hips high. Your aim here is to form the shape of an upside-down “V.”
  4. If you have the flexibility in your hamstring muscles, straighten your legs and let your heels drop down toward the floor while maintaining the length in your spine.
  5. If you notice your spine start to curve as you straighten your legs, bend your knees enough so that you can keep the spine long.
  6. Hold for 5 breaths.

Bridge Pose

  1. Lie on your back.
  2. Bend both knees and position your feet hip-width apart with your knees stacked over your ankles.
  3. Place your arms on either side of your body with the palms of your hands turned down to the ground. Spread your fingers wide.
  4. Lengthen the skin of your tailbone toward the front of your mat.
  5. Lift your hips up and hold the pose for 5 breaths.

Tree Pose

  1. Stand up straight. Shift your weight onto the left foot, keeping the inner part of your left foot firmly on the floor, and bend your right knee.
  2. Draw your right foot up and place the sole against your inner left thigh, inner calf muscle, or inner ankle with your toes touching the floor.
  3. Place your hands on the top rim of your pelvis to make sure that it’s parallel to the floor.
  4. Lengthen your tailbone toward the floor.
  5. Firmly press the sole of the right foot against the inner thigh, calf, or ankle, and resist with the outer left leg.
  6. Raise your arms straight above your head. Ensure that you keep your shoulders relaxed.
  7. Hold for 5 breaths before changing to the other side.

Spine Twist

  1. Lie on your back.
  2. Hug both knees in toward yourself with your feet off the ground.
  3. Place your arms in a “T” position, with the palms of your hands turned up toward the ceiling.
  4. Let both knees drop down toward the right side of your mat.
  5. Keep your gaze looking toward the ceiling, or turn to face the opposite direction of your knees.
  6. Hold for 5 breaths before coming to the other side.

Cat Cow

  1. Get on your hands and knees. Your wrists should be underneath your shoulders and your knees underneath your hips.
  2. Balance your weight evenly on all fours.
  3. Inhale and look up, letting your stomach point down towards the mat.
  4. Then exhale and tuck your chin into your chest, curving your spin up towards the ceiling.
  5. Be awareness of your body and your breath as repeat these movements.
  6. Continue this fluid movement for 5 breaths.

Seated Forward Fold

  1. Sit on the ground with your legs straight out in front of you. If you have tight hamstrings, bend your knees.
  2. Keep your feet flexed with your toes pointing toward the ceiling.
  3. Sit up tall, lengthening through your spine.
  4. Leading with your chest, keep your spine long as you fold forward.
  5. Place your hands in a comfortable position on your legs.
  6. Hold for 5 breaths.
